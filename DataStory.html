BACK TO BITE US IN THE BUTT

By Jayden Skuladotir
The frequency of shark attacks has been rising considerably the past few years and the risk of being attacked by a shark is higher than it has ever been in history. The records of shark attacks for this story range between 1898 and early October of 2022.
The question is why?

The obvious answer for this rise in attacks is population growth.

Human's exponential population growth simply means that there are more humans on Earth for sharks to attack.

This is what most articles and news stories will posit as the main reason for the increase in incidents.

But…that can't be it, right? Especially since most shark attacks occur less than 100 feet from shore. Sharks come near shore for various reasons, and it is not because the human population is getting larger.
Why Sharks Travel Near Shore

Sharks are cold-blooded creatures. They will migrate to areas with warmer water temperatures.

The overall warming of the ocean's water draws more sharks towards shore.

In fact, the change in the ocean's temperature has been so drastic that sharks have abandoned their seasonal migrations.

Sharks also often go near shores for nursing purposes.

They may feel they are under threat with so many people enroaching on their territory.

Most of the time, however, they are near shore searching for food.

Aside from being ideal hunting grounds, researchers also speculate that the fish carcasses left behind by fishing industries attract them closer to shore.

Sharks are not craving human flesh. In fact, they very much dislike us and would rather not eat us.

A majority of shark attacks are of the “bite and release” nature.

Humans floating out in the ocean can be mistaken as prey.

The shark will attack, realize that it has attacked a human. Since sharks don't fancy humans, it lets go and does not attack further.

This reality highly contrasts horror movies about sharks where they seem to have undeniable bloodlust for the stranded persons in the ocean.

Combined, the USA, AUSTRALIA, and SOUTH AFRICA account for over half of the total recorded shark attack incidents. Again, it is easy to posit population growth as the primary reason for the increase of shark attacks, but there are other factors at play here that need to be considered.

The USA, AUSTRALIA, & SOUTH AFRICA: What's Different?
Florida Attack Timeline 1970-2022

While the USA composes about 38% of shark attacks in total, Florida is a major hotspot.

New Smyrna Beach in Volusia County by itself has a total of almost 200 attacks between 1899 and 2022.

There is no denying that population plays a role in the rise of shark attacks.

However, if the rise in shark attacks was solely due to population growth, it'd be expected to see more recorded encounters in other locations like California: the second most shark attacked ridden state.

So, why does Florida have so many attacks?
One possible explanation for the incidents is the fact that the acidity of the water on Florida's coasts is considerably low.
Ocean Acidity

Acidity is measured in various ways.

The most known metric for measuring acidity is the pH scale. Lower values indicate higher acidity.

As of today, the ocean's average pH is about 8.1.

Since 1800, the ocean's average pH has decreased by ~0.1

While this change does not seem drastic, the 0.1 drop indicates ~30% overall increased water acidity.

Aragonite saturation is also used to measure acidity and it is sometimes more telling than the pH scale.

Aragonite saturation is the measure of calcium carbonate concentration in the water. It is a measure of the levels of carbon dioxide. Marine life uses calcium carbonate to build and maintain their skeletons and shells.

A lower aragonite saturation means that there is more carbon dioxide in the water.

An aragonite saturation greater than 4.0 is ideal. It is directly linked with pH: The lower the saturation, the lower the pH, the more acidic the water.

Global Aragonite Saturation 2020

Research has shown that sharks will avoid acidic waters, even if they smell prey.

The greater saturation of aragonite of Florida's waters (around 3.5-4.0) indicates a higher pH. This will have more sharks hanging out in the Caribbean Sea, the Gulf of Mexico, and nearby Florida shores.

Florida's neighboring states like South and North Carolina don't have the luxury of having as high of a saturation or temperature (yet) on their coasts, so less attacks tend to happen in these areas.

In addition to this, more adequate levels of saturation are present on the shores of South Africa and Australia, the countries that rank second and third in having the most shark attacks.

Less shark attacks occur in areas where the saturation and, by extention, the pH, is lower. Nonetheless, the numbers have been rising globally, regardless of acidity. Once again, population is the immediate culprit. But acidic water may affect the risk of shark attacks too, because general shark behavior has shown to be different in highly acidic water.

Their hunting abilities are decreased, meaning it is potentially more likely that sharks will mistake humans for actual prey.

They will surface more often in highly acidic water. This brings them closer to human activity more often.

Furthermore, the increased temperature and concentration of carbon dioxide in the ocean's water has been linked to hindering the development of sharks' brains, impacting behavior and decision making.

Human activity has a very real influence on sharks. The impacts that humans are making on the ocean may be increasing the risk of shark encounters in various and subtle ways.

Conclusion
The numbers look big and scary, but it is important to keep in mind that shark attacks are still extremely rare. Remember that humans kill millions of sharks every year. In fact, the sharks in our oceans are at risk of extinction. It is completely understandable to be afraid of getting attacked by a shark. One moment you are having a lovely day at the beach, the next you are bleeding out because a shark mistook you for food. But we cannot deny that human activity is impacting sharks and their behavior, leading to increased risk of attacks. While it is scary, the fear should not be turned towards sharks themselves. Ultimately, we should be afraid of our own actions because our activity is making the risk of being attacked by a shark higher than it has ever been in history.
